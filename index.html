Project

Restaurant Best Food Award
& Attraction Analysis

Investigated whether number of reviews correlates with higher food ratings or receiving Best Food Awards across US states, using Python statistical analysis. Examined how this relationship varies by geography and what it means for restaurants and consumers.

Tools
Python, Statistics
Methods
Correlation analysis, distribution skewness, state-level aggregation
Scope
50 US states · restaurant-level data
Research Question
Do more reviews = higher ratings or Best Food Awards?

Confounding Factors Considered

  • Population size directly impacts restaurant foot traffic and review volume — higher-population states naturally accumulate more reviews
  • State-level competition density affects how review counts distribute across restaurants
  • These factors can inflate averages and obscure the true reviews-to-quality relationship

Conclusions & Implications

  • Weak correlation: more reviews does not reliably predict higher food ratings
  • Weak correlation between review volume and receiving a Best Food Award
  • Restaurants should prioritize quality and experience over chasing review counts
  • Consumers should weight rating consistency and award status over raw review numbers

Perceived vs Actual Inflation
Project

Perceived vs Actual Inflation
& Household Spending

Examined how perceived inflation (SCE — Survey of Consumer Expectations) and actual inflation (PCE — Personal Consumption Expenditures) influence household spending behaviour in the U.S. in the short-term post-COVID period. The core question: do consumers spend based on what they believe will happen to prices, or what is actually happening?

Tools
Python — numpy, pandas, matplotlib
Methods
Linear Regression (ML), MSE, R-squared, Correlation
Data Sources
SCE (Perceived Inflation) · PCE (Actual Inflation) · EXP (Spending Change)
Period
Post-COVID short-term window — U.S.

Why This Matters

  • Perception can shape consumer behaviour, business decisions, and policy just as strongly as actual inflation
  • When consumers expect prices to rise, they change spending even if real inflation is stable — influencing demand and pricing strategies
  • Policymakers react to these behavioural shifts when setting interest rates
  • Understanding whether perception or reality drives spending is essential to forecasting economic activity

Conclusion

  • Perceived inflation (SCE) shows only weak positive correlation with spending (r = 0.1775)
  • Actual inflation (PCE) shows a stronger negative correlation with spending (r = −0.5812)
  • Post-COVID consumers still tend to spend based on what they believe will happen to prices
  • Key insight: perception often outweighs reality in driving economic behaviour

USD/CNY FX Rate vs China Exports
Project · Macroeconomic & Data Research Analyst

USD/CNY FX Rate vs
China's Exports to the US

Built an interactive Python dashboard using API-sourced data to explore the relationship between the USD/CNY exchange rate, the Fed Funds Rate, and China's export volumes to the United States. The dashboard supports exploratory analysis through two complementary visualizations and interactive filters across date range, FX rate, and Fed Funds rate.

Tools
Python — Pandas, Plotly, Dash
Data Sources
FRED API — USD/CNY, Fed Funds Rate, China–US Export volumes
Dashboard Features
Interactive filters · Date range slider · FX & Fed Funds range sliders
Period
2000–2005 (pre-RMB reform era)

Key Finding — Inverse Relationship

  • USD/CNY FX rate and China's export volumes to the US show an inverse relationship — counterintuitive to standard trade theory
  • Conventional logic: a weaker CNY (higher USD/CNY) should boost exports by making Chinese goods cheaper
  • Instead, export volumes trend upward even as the FX rate remains pegged and relatively stable
  • This suggests China's exports are not primarily price-driven in this period

Why This Happened — Structural Shift

  • China had shifted away from commodity and low-cost manufacturing toward price-inelastic products — electronics, medical devices, and technology goods
  • These categories are demand-driven rather than price-driven: US buyers import them regardless of exchange rate movements
  • Total export dollar volume grew without relying on a competitive exchange rate advantage
  • Insight: China's export competitiveness was increasingly structural, not monetary
